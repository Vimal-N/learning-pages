Why AI, Why Now?

Three things changed at once in 2022–2023, and that's why AI is suddenly everywhere.

AI itself is not new. Spell-check, spam filters, face recognition, and recommendation algorithms are all AI. What changed recently isn't AI in general — it's one specific type of AI called Large Language Models (LLMs), which became dramatically more capable in a very short time.

What changed
  • Scale: Models trained on essentially the entire internet, not curated datasets
  • Architecture: The Transformer (2017) unlocked a new kind of pattern recognition
  • RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback made outputs safe enough to ship
What stayed the same
  • AI cannot "think". It predicts tokens.
  • It has no memory between conversations (by default)
  • It can be confidently wrong — and it won't always tell you
  • Garbage in, garbage out — the quality of your prompt matters

The honest version: GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini are all "just" very large autocomplete systems trained on huge amounts of human text. They are genuinely impressive at text generation — but they are not thinking. That distinction matters for how you use them safely.

How LLMs Actually Work (No Maths)

Tokens, prediction, and why "hallucination" is a feature of the mechanism, not a bug in the software.

An LLM receives your text as a sequence of tokens (roughly: words and word-parts), then predicts what token should come next — over and over until it stops. That's the whole thing. The magic is in how good it is at that prediction, because it was trained on an enormous sample of human writing.

The reason LLMs sometimes make up facts — called hallucination — is not a bug that gets patched. It's the mechanism. The model predicts what text sounds plausible, and sometimes plausible text is factually wrong. This is why you review what it generates rather than copying it blindly.

The key insight: An LLM doesn't know what's true. It knows what patterns of text have appeared together across billions of documents. For code, that's powerful. For facts (API names, library versions, specific URLs), always verify.

Tokens & the Context Window

Why longer isn't always better, and why the AI "forgets" at the start of long conversations.

Everything the LLM processes — your message, its previous responses, any attached files — must fit inside a context window, measured in tokens. Think of it as a limited-length piece of paper the model can "see" at any moment.

~1 token
≈ 1 short word, or half a long word. "test" = 1 token. "authentication" = 3 tokens.
4K tokens
≈ 3,000 words (this entire session guide). Early GPT-3.5 limit.
200K tokens
≈ a large novel (Claude). Modern models have dramatically larger windows.

When the conversation fills the context window, the model either truncates early messages or summarises them — it doesn't have selective memory like a person. In practice this means: a new chat for a new task is often better than one very long conversation.

For Copilot Chat in VS Code: Each new Chat panel starts fresh. The model doesn't remember what you asked yesterday, or even earlier in a different tab. This is why context references like @workspace and #file: exist — they re-inject what the model needs to know.

What AI Is Good At

The patterns it genuinely accelerates — and why QA work maps onto them well.

AI excels here
  • Pattern completion: If it's seen thousands of examples, it can generate a new one confidently
  • Boilerplate: Page Object classes, test fixtures, data factories
  • Translation: "Convert this manual test script into a Playwright test"
  • Explanation: "Why is this Playwright selector fragile?" — great answers
  • Naming and refactoring: "Rename this function and update all call sites"
  • First draft generation: Starting from a blank file is the hardest part
  • Error messages: "I got this error — what does it mean?"
AI struggles here
  • Your specific app: It doesn't know your selectors, your URLs, your business logic
  • Latest library versions: Training data has a cutoff; docs change
  • Novel problems: Things that don't follow known patterns
  • Long sequential reasoning: Complex multi-step logic it hasn't seen before
  • Definitive facts: Always verify API names, version numbers, config keys
Try This — 3 minutes

Open ChatGPT, Claude, or any AI chat tool. Ask: "Explain what a Playwright locator is and give me three examples of robust locators for a login form." Then ask: "What's the exact CSS selector for the login button on the NHS website?" Notice the difference in how confident (or appropriately cautious) the response is. That contrast shows you exactly where AI knowledge is reliable vs. guessed.

Why QA Engineers Are Well-Placed

The skills you already have are exactly what makes someone good at working with AI.

There's a common assumption that "AI is for developers". It's wrong. QA engineers are often better at extracting value from AI tools — not despite their role, but because of it. Here's why:

1
You already think in test cases
Writing precise instructions with expected outcomes is exactly how you prompt an AI. "Given X, when Y, then Z" is both a test case format and an excellent prompt structure.
2
You know how to verify outputs
Your natural instinct is to check things. That instinct is exactly the right response to AI-generated code. Most people accept outputs; you'll review them.
3
You understand edge cases
When you ask AI to generate tests, you'll spot what it missed. "What about empty fields? What about session expiry?" — these are QA instincts developers often don't have.
4
You're used to ambiguous requirements
Working with unclear specs — asking clarifying questions, making assumptions explicit — is exactly how you get good AI outputs. You've been practising this for years.

What Prompting Actually Is

Not magic words. Not a trick. Just good communication — with a machine that's very literal.

Prompting is the practice of writing instructions that reliably get you the output you want from an AI. The word sounds technical but the skill is not: it's about being precise, providing enough context, and specifying what you actually need.

The gap between a poor prompt and a good one is almost never about knowing secret tricks. It's about not assuming the AI knows things it can't know:

Weak prompt
"Write a test for the login page."
The AI has no idea what your login page looks like. It will guess. The output will be structurally correct but use imaginary selectors and your URL.
Strong prompt
"Using Playwright and TypeScript, write a test that navigates to https://example.com/login, enters 'user@test.com' in #email and 'wrongpassword' in #password, clicks button[type=submit], and asserts the error message 'Invalid credentials' is visible."
Specific selectors, specific URL, specific assertion. The AI can now generate code you can actually run.

Common Misconceptions — Cleared Up

Things people believe about AI that get in the way of using it well.

❌ "AI will replace QA engineers"
AI generates code. It does not understand your product, your users, your risk tolerance, or your release process. It is a power tool, not a replacement. QA engineers who use AI well become significantly more productive — they are not eliminated.
❌ "AI is always right if it sounds confident"
Confidence is a function of how the output was generated, not of accuracy. LLMs produce text that sounds authoritative even when the content is wrong. Review everything, especially specific claims about APIs, version numbers, or security.
❌ "You need to be a developer to use AI tools"
Natural language is the interface. You can ask AI to explain code, generate documentation, convert requirements to test cases, or spot problems — without writing a single line of code yourself.
❌ "The AI remembers what I told it before"
It doesn't — not between sessions, and in some tools not even between conversations. Always provide the context it needs, every time. This is why tools like Copilot's @workspace exist.

AI in the QA Workflow

Where these tools slot in — and where they don't.

AI tools fit into QA work at several points in the workflow. Understanding where they help (and where you still own the judgement call) is the difference between using AI well and using it dangerously.

AI assists with
  • Converting acceptance criteria into test cases
  • Generating Playwright test code from descriptions
  • Explaining why a test is failing
  • Refactoring repetitive test code
  • Generating test data variations
  • Reviewing test coverage gaps
  • Writing documentation from code
You still own
  • Deciding what to test and what risk to accept
  • Verifying that generated tests actually test what you intend
  • Catch the "always-passing test" trap (a test that can never fail)
  • Understanding your specific app behaviour
  • Release sign-off and quality judgement

The "always-passing test" trap: AI can generate a test that passes every time — because it asserts something that's always true or never actually reaches the failing condition. Your instinct as a QA engineer — "does this test actually prove anything?" — is your defence against this. Session 07 covers it in detail.
